Driving E-Filing Adoption: A Behavioral Science Approach to Digital Transformation

Project Overview

This large-scale behavioral intervention study focused on significantly increasing payroll tax e-filing adoption among businesses and tax preparers. Using rigorous experimental design and behavioral science principles, we implemented targeted communication strategies to shift users from traditional paper filing to efficient digital platforms.

Research Design Highlights

  • Scale: 22,500 initial filers (90,000 total observations via Latin square design)
  • Method: Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT) with three arms: Postcard, Letter/Flyer, Control
  • Design: Extra-period Latin squares sequence for maximum statistical power
  • Duration: 4 quarterly filing periods with phased interventions
  • Collaboration: Cross-functional team including UX/Design, Communications, Legal, and Data Analytics

Key Research Findings

  • Postcards Outperformed: Consistent and significantly higher effectiveness across all filer segments
  • Behavioral Science Validated: Simple, salient "nudges" proved more effective than information-heavy approaches
  • Measurable Impact: 5.8-7.1 percentage point increases in e-filing probability
  • Cost-Effective Solution: Lower-cost intervention showed superior results

Intervention Design & Materials

Working closely with UX/UI designers and communications teams, we created targeted materials based on behavioral science principles. Each intervention emphasized the core benefits of e-filing: Secure, Easy, and Accurate.

Taxpayer Flyer
Detailed information approach with clear benefits and user-friendly design for individual business owners.
Preparer Flyer
Professional messaging focused on practice efficiency and enhanced client service for tax professionals.
Taxpayer Postcard
Concise nudge approach with immediate visual impact and clear call-to-action for rapid processing.
Preparer Postcard
Direct professional message emphasizing efficiency and reliability for tax service providers.
Average Marginal Effects
Average Marginal Effects demonstrate postcards consistently outperformed letters across all filer segments, with increases of 5.8-7.1 percentage points in e-filing probability.
Odds Ratios Analysis
Odds Ratios reinforce AME findings, showing postcards increased e-filing odds by approximately 20% across all segments compared to control.

Research Methodology

Rigorous Experimental Design

Our approach employed a sophisticated extra-period Latin squares sequence within a Randomized Controlled Trial framework. This within-subject design significantly enhanced statistical efficiency, allowing detection of smaller yet meaningful effects with greater confidence.

Behavioral Science Foundation

Interventions were grounded in established behavioral science principles, particularly the effectiveness of simple, timely nudges over information-heavy approaches for busy professional audiences. The design emphasized clarity, actionability, and trust-building.

Statistical Analysis

We utilized probit regression models ideal for binary outcomes (e-file vs. paper), estimating treatment effects on probabilities while controlling for demographic and business characteristics. This approach ensured we isolated the causal impact of our communications.
